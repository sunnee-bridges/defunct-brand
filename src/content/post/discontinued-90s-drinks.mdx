The 1990s were the most experimental decade in American beverage history. Soda companies tried clear colas, citrus drinks with twice the caffeine, juice bottles with floating gelatin spheres, and anti-brand sodas designed to appeal to Gen X cynicism. Energy drinks were just emerging. Premium sparkling water was becoming a thing. Every few months something new and strange appeared in the cooler at the gas station.

Most of it disappeared just as fast.

Some of these drinks were genuine innovations that deserved to survive. Others were novelties that burned bright for one summer and could never justify a second. All of them left a specific kind of impression on anyone who grew up drinking them, the kind of taste memory that comes back sharply when someone mentions the name.

Here is what actually happened to ten of the most missed discontinued drinks from the 90s.

---

## 1. Surge (1997 to 2003)

Surge was Coca-Cola's direct response to Mountain Dew, which had been eating into Coke's market share for years by owning the high-caffeine citrus soda space. Coke developed Surge specifically to compete and launched it in 1997 with an aggressive marketing campaign aimed at teenage boys that leaned hard into extreme sports and maximum energy imagery.

It worked, for a while. Surge developed a genuinely devoted following, particularly among kids who wanted the caffeine hit of Mountain Dew with what many described as a slightly more complex citrus flavor. It became the unofficial fuel of early internet culture and LAN parties in the late 1990s.

Coke discontinued it in 2003 as sales declined, but the fan response was unlike almost anything the beverage industry had seen. A dedicated Facebook group called Surge Movement accumulated hundreds of thousands of followers demanding its return. Fans even purchased a billboard near Coca-Cola's Atlanta headquarters in 2013 to make the demand visible.

Coke brought Surge back in 2014 as an Amazon exclusive, which was itself a notable moment in how brands thought about direct-to-consumer nostalgia products. It eventually returned to limited retail distribution. The current version exists but has never recaptured the cultural presence of the original run.

## 2. Crystal Pepsi (1992 to 1993)

Crystal Pepsi arrived during a specific cultural moment when "clear" had become a marketing shorthand for pure, clean, and modern. Pepsi positioned it as a caffeine-free cola that looked nothing like cola, betting that the visual novelty combined with a familiar taste would appeal to health-conscious consumers who still wanted soda.

The launch was enormous. Crystal Pepsi debuted during Super Bowl XXVII in 1993 with a Van Halen commercial and generated massive initial sales. Within months it had become one of the most recognized new products in the country.

The problem was that the taste did not quite match the visual promise. Crystal Pepsi tasted like Pepsi without the caramel color, which meant it tasted like a slightly off version of something familiar rather than something genuinely new. Consumers tried it out of curiosity and most did not come back for a second purchase.

7UP tried to accelerate the decline by launching a "right cola, wrong color" campaign for 7UP Gold that directly mocked Crystal Pepsi's premise. Pepsi discontinued it by the end of 1993, less than a year after launch. It has returned as a limited nostalgia release several times since 2015, which is perhaps the most honest assessment of what it always was: something people are curious about rather than something they actually want to drink regularly.

## 3. Orbitz (1997 to 1998)

Orbitz was a fruit-flavored drink with small gelatin spheres suspended throughout the liquid. The spheres did not dissolve and did not sink. They just floated there, held in place by a system involving gellan gum that kept the liquid viscous enough to suspend them while still looking clear. The bottles looked like lava lamps.

It was one of the most visually distinctive beverages ever sold in mainstream American retail and it generated enormous curiosity when it launched in 1997. People bought it to look at it as much as to drink it.

The problem was that the gelatin spheres felt deeply wrong in your mouth. The novelty of the visual effect did not translate into an enjoyable drinking experience for most people. Orbitz sold on curiosity and then could not convert curious first-time buyers into repeat customers because the core experience of drinking it was off-putting.

It was discontinued in 1998 after roughly a year on shelves. Original sealed bottles are now collector's items that regularly sell on eBay for significant amounts, which is a fitting end for a product that was always more about appearance than taste.

## 4. Snapple Elements (1999 to 2002)

Snapple Elements was four drinks named after natural elements, each in a distinctive wide-mouth glass bottle that looked more like a decorative object than a beverage. Rain was agave cactus. Sun was starfruit, orange, and pineapple. Fire featured dragonfruit. Earth was an herbal blend.

The bottles became the story. They were wider at the mouth than standard glass bottles, which made them feel substantial and collectible. People saved them and reused them. They became a symbol of a specific moment in premium beverage aesthetics.

What ended Elements was a logistics problem rather than a consumer preference problem. The wide-mouth bottles could not fit in standard vending machines, cutting off a major distribution channel. They were heavier and more expensive to ship than standard glass. Each of those factors eroded the economics of the line until Snapple could not justify continuing it.

The demand never went away. Snapple Elements petitions have circulated online for over twenty years. It remains one of the most consistently requested discontinued beverages in any nostalgia discussion.

## 5. Fruitopia (1994 to 2003 in the US)

Fruitopia was Coca-Cola's attempt to compete with Snapple in the premium juice drink market and it arrived with some of the most distinctive branding of the decade. The bottles featured psychedelic artwork and the flavor names were written in a New Age register that felt very specifically of its moment. Strawberry Passion Awareness. Citrus Consciousness. The marketing leaned into the idea that choosing a beverage could be a lifestyle statement.

It found real success in the mid-1990s and became a fixture in school cafeterias and convenience stores. The branding that felt fresh in 1994 started to feel dated by the late 1990s as the cultural moment it was designed for passed. Health trends were moving away from the high-sugar juice drink category. Snapple itself was struggling for similar reasons.

Coke discontinued Fruitopia in the US in 2003. The brand survived in Canada, Australia, and New Zealand where it is still sold today, which makes it an interesting case of a product that failed in its home market but found lasting success abroad.

## 6. OK Soda (1993 to 1995)

OK Soda was one of the most fascinating brand experiments in American beverage history and it never made it out of test markets. Coca-Cola developed it specifically for Generation X, a demographic that marketing researchers had identified as deeply skeptical of traditional advertising. The strategy was to build a brand around that skepticism rather than fight it.

The cans featured artwork by underground comic artists Daniel Clowes and Charles Burns, whose work had almost no precedent in mainstream consumer products. The marketing leaned into irony and indifference. The tagline was "What's the point of OK? Well, what's the point of anything?" There was a 1-800 number on the can that played a manifesto when you called it.

The flavor itself was described as a blend of fruit and cola notes that most people found unremarkable. The brand concept was the product as much as the liquid inside.

OK Soda never expanded beyond a handful of test markets before Coke pulled it in 1995. Whether it would have worked at national scale is one of the great unanswered questions in 90s brand history. Original cans are now genuine collectibles. The brand is studied in marketing programs as an unusually early example of anti-marketing strategy.

## 7. Clearly Canadian (peak 1990s)

Clearly Canadian was a Canadian sparkling water brand that found its biggest cultural moment in the United States during the early 1990s. The drinks came in jewel-toned glass bottles with embossed lettering and fruit flavors like Wild Cherry, Country Raspberry, and Orchard Peach that tasted genuinely different from standard carbonated beverages.

At a time when the premium sparkling water category barely existed in American retail, Clearly Canadian felt sophisticated and European in a way that appealed to consumers who wanted something more interesting than soda but more flavorful than plain water. It was ahead of its time in ways that ultimately worked against it.

Distribution problems plagued the brand through the late 1990s and early 2000s. Maintaining glass bottle supply chains across North American retail was expensive and complicated. Sales declined and Clearly Canadian largely disappeared from US shelves by the mid-2000s.

A Kickstarter campaign in 2015 raised enough funding to revive limited production and the response demonstrated that genuine demand still existed. Distribution remained limited and the revival never reached the scale of the original. The brand still exists in reduced form but has never recaptured its 1990s presence.

## 8. Jolt Cola (1985, peaked in the 1990s)

Jolt Cola technically launched in 1985 but its cultural peak was the 1990s, when its tagline of "all the sugar and twice the caffeine" resonated with a generation of students, gamers, and anyone who needed to stay awake without the social acceptability of coffee.

It was the direct ancestor of the energy drink category. Red Bull launched in the US in 1997 and Monster arrived in 2002, and both of them owed a conceptual debt to what Jolt had established. The difference was that Red Bull and Monster were built from the ground up as energy drink brands with the marketing infrastructure to match, while Jolt was a cola that had been retrofitted into that role.

Jolt went through a series of ownership changes and reformulations that gradually eroded its identity. A battery-shaped can redesign in the mid-2000s briefly gave it a second life before the brand faded again. It has had scattered regional availability since but has never been nationally distributed in a consistent way. What made Jolt culturally significant was the specific moment it occupied before the energy drink category existed to replace it.

## 9. Snapple Whipper Snapple (1998 to 2000)

Whipper Snapple was Snapple's entry into the bottled smoothie category before that category properly existed. The line offered shelf-stable blended fruit drinks in Strawberry Banana, Orange Dream, and Peach Mango that were thicker and creamier than standard juice drinks.

The concept was genuinely innovative for a grocery store shelf product. The execution was commercially difficult. Shelf-stable smoothies required different production and packaging than Snapple's core products. The smoothie bar category was about to explode with Jamba Juice and similar chains, and consumers who wanted a smoothie increasingly preferred a fresh one made in front of them.

Whipper Snapple was discontinued by 2000 as Snapple refocused its lineup. The timing is notable because the bottled smoothie category it was trying to create became enormous within a few years of its discontinuation. Brands like Odwalla and Naked Juice built major businesses on nearly identical premises.

## 10. Orbitz (Already covered above) → replacing with: Zipplez / **Hi-C Ecto Cooler (1987 to 2001)**

Hi-C Ecto Cooler launched in 1987 as a tie-in with the Ghostbusters animated series, specifically designed to feature Slimer as the mascot. The tangerine citrus flavor was genuinely good and it outlasted its promotional origins by over a decade, surviving long after Slimer had faded from cultural relevance.

Coca-Cola finally discontinued it in 2001 when they lost the Ghostbusters licensing rights. The drink that had been intended as a two-year promotional product had become a beloved staple of school lunches and after-school snacking for an entire generation.

When the Ghostbusters reboot launched in 2016 Coca-Cola brought Ecto Cooler back as a limited promotional tie-in. The response was massive. Cases sold out immediately. People were paying significant premiums on secondary markets for a carton of juice drink. That reaction said everything about how strongly the original had embedded itself in the memory of people who grew up drinking it.

## Why the 90s Produced So Many Experiments

The 1990s were unusual in ways that created the conditions for this kind of beverage experimentation. The economy was strong through most of the decade, which gave large companies the budget to fund speculative product launches. The generation coming of age was large and culturally influential, which made reaching them a priority. And the competitive landscape in beverages had not yet consolidated around the clear winners it has today.

Every major soda company was trying to find the next big thing. The result was a decade of products that ranged from genuinely innovative to deeply strange, most of which did not survive contact with the realities of shelf space, distribution economics, and consumer habits that would not sustain novelty past the first purchase.

The drinks that are most missed are generally the ones that were ahead of their time in some way. Clearly Canadian anticipated the premium sparkling water boom. Whipper Snapple anticipated the bottled smoothie category. Snapple Elements built a design-forward brand around functional beverages before that was a standard strategy. They failed not because the ideas were wrong but because the market was not ready or the economics were not yet workable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why did so many 90s drinks get discontinued?**
The combination of corporate ownership changes, limited retail shelf space, shifting consumer preferences toward less sugar, and the rise of energy drinks and craft beverages squeezed out most of the experimental products from the decade. Products that sold on novelty rarely built the repeat purchase habits needed to justify permanent shelf space.

**Did Surge ever come back?**
Yes. Surge returned in 2014 initially as an Amazon exclusive after a sustained fan campaign. It has since had limited retail distribution but has never returned to the scale of its original run.

**Is Crystal Pepsi available anywhere?**
Pepsi has brought Crystal Pepsi back several times as limited nostalgia releases, most recently in 2015 and 2016. It is not currently in regular production but tends to reappear periodically.

**Why does Fruitopia still exist in Canada but not the US?**
Canadian consumer preferences and retail relationships supported continued sales after the US discontinuation. The brand was maintained internationally even after Coke pulled it from American shelves in 2003.

**Are original Orbitz bottles worth anything?**
Sealed original Orbitz bottles in good condition sell regularly on eBay for between twenty and one hundred dollars depending on flavor and condition. The gelatin spheres inside are no longer safe to consume but the bottles themselves are genuine collector's items.
