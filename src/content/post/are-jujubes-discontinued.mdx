## A Fun, Friendly Look at the History of Jujubes

Jujubes were once a classic movie‑theater candy. They came in a bright yellow box and were known for being chewy, colorful, and long‑lasting. People loved them because one box could last through a whole movie.

The candy was created by the Heide Candy Company in 1920. Even though they were named after the jujube fruit, they didn't actually taste like it. Instead, they came in fruity flavors like lemon, lime, orange, cherry, and violet.

Their texture was firm and sticky. That made them famous for getting stuck in teeth and for lasting forever. Some people loved that. Others... not so much.

### What Made Them Special

Jujubes weren't just candy. They were a movie‑theater tradition. For decades, that bright yellow box sat right next to the popcorn and Milk Duds at the concession stand.

One box could last a two‑hour film. That was part of the appeal. You didn't rush through them. You worked for them.

The flavors were simple:
- 🟡 Lemon
- 🟢 Lime
- 🟠 Orange
- 🔴 Cherry
- 🟣 Violet

The violet flavor was a lot. People either loved it or skipped it entirely.

### The Texture Was Polarizing

Jujubes were firm, dense, and chewy. Some people joked that they were tough enough to pull out a filling.

That reputation followed the candy everywhere. It became part of the joke, and part of the charm.

<a id="why-hard-to-find" />
## Why Did Jujubes Become So Hard to Find?

There's no single dramatic reason. It was a slow fade, driven mostly by ownership changes and shrinking support.

### 1. Ownership Changed Many Times

Jujubes had a complicated corporate history. Every time the brand changed hands, it got a little less attention.

**Heide Candy Company (1920–1995)**<br />
Heide created Jujubes and built them into a theater staple. Under Heide, the candy had a real identity and a loyal following.

**Hershey (1995–2002)**<br />
Hershey acquired Heide in 1995. Jujubes stayed in production, but they were never a priority brand. Big chocolate companies don't always know what to do with old‑school chewy candy.

**Farley's & Sathers (2002–2012)**<br />
Hershey sold Jujubes and other Heide brands to Farley's & Sathers in 2002. Distribution got smaller. The candy became harder to find in mainstream stores.

**Ferrara Candy Company (2012–present)**<br />
Farley's & Sathers merged with Ferrara Pan in 2012. Jujubes landed fully under Ferrara by 2017. Production continued, but only in very small amounts.

Each handoff meant less marketing, less shelf space, and fewer stores carrying the candy.

### 2. No Marketing Support

No one was really fighting for Jujubes.

Each new owner had a full portfolio of brands to manage. Jujubes was a legacy product with an aging fan base. There were no new commercials, no social media pushes, no big retail deals.

Without visibility, shoppers stopped looking for them. Stores stopped stocking what shoppers weren't asking for.

### 3. Candy Tastes Changed

The candy world changed a lot between the 1920s and the 2000s. Consumers moved toward softer textures, brighter flavors, and things that were easier to eat. Jujubes were firm, old‑fashioned, and required effort. That worked for decades, but it worked less well in a world full of Sour Patch Kids and Trolli worms.

Younger generations never got the chance to fall in love with Jujubes. By the time they were buying their own candy, Jujubes weren't on the shelf anymore.

## Timeline of Jujubes

**1920**<br />
Jujubes are created by the Heide Candy Company and quickly become a popular movie‑theater candy across the United States.

**1995**<br />
Hershey acquires the Heide Candy Company, including Jujubes. Production continues, but the brand receives limited attention under its new owner.

**2002**<br />
Hershey sells Jujubes and other Heide brands to Farley's & Sathers. Distribution begins to shrink and the candy becomes harder to find in mainstream retail.

**2005**<br />
Jujubes become noticeably scarce. Movie theaters begin dropping the candy from concession stands.

**2012**<br />
Farley's & Sathers merges with Ferrara Pan, forming Ferrara Candy Company. Ownership of Jujubes shifts again.

**2017**<br />
Jujubes land fully under Ferrara Candy Company. Production remains very limited.

**2018–present**<br />
Limited production continues under Ferrara. Most fans describe Jujubes as effectively discontinued because they are so rarely found in stores.

## Can You Still Find Jujubes Today?

Jujubes are not officially discontinued. Ferrara Candy Company still produces them. But production is so limited that most people will never see them on a store shelf. Online is your best bet — Amazon, Candy Warehouse, and specialty retro candy stores are the most reliable places to look.

If you order online, check the date and the seller's reviews. Limited production means limited freshness, and old Jujubes are even chewier than fresh ones.

## Why People Still Miss Jujubes

### 1. The Movie Theater Connection

Jujubes didn't just taste good. They were part of the movie‑going ritual. Dark theaters, sticky floors, that bright yellow box. For a lot of people, Jujubes *are* the memory of going to the movies as a kid.

Nothing else has filled that specific spot.

### 2. The Chewing Was Part of the Fun

Other candies were easy. Jujubes were a workout. You bought one box and you were set for the whole film. There was something satisfying about that slow, steady chew.

### 3. The Flavors Were One of a Kind

Violet‑flavored candy doesn't really exist anywhere else. Neither does that exact combination of dense chewiness and quiet fruit flavor. Jujubes were quieter and more stubborn than anything on shelves today.

Some people prefer stubborn.

### 4. The Nostalgia Is Real

For Boomers and Gen X, Jujubes bring back a very specific kind of memory. Not just of the candy, but of who they were with, where they were sitting, and what was on the screen. That's a hard thing to replace with a bag of Haribo.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Who made Jujubes originally?
They were first made by the Heide Candy Company in 1920.

### What exactly were Jujubes?
Jujubes were chewy, fruit‑flavored candies sold in a small yellow theater box. They were known for their firm texture, long‑lasting chew, and tendency to stick to your teeth. Flavors included lemon, lime, orange, cherry, and violet.

### When did Jujubes disappear from stores?
There's no official end date. They became very hard to find starting in the mid‑2000s, after the brand changed hands from Hershey to Farley's & Sathers and distribution got much smaller.

### Where were Jujubes most popular?
They were especially popular in U.S. movie theaters for many decades. The small theater‑box format made them a concession stand staple for generations.

### Why did Jujubes become so hard to find?
There's no single confirmed reason. Multiple ownership changes reduced marketing and distribution support over time. Consumer tastes also shifted, and Jujubes never adapted to keep up.

### How can I find Jujubes today?
Your best chance is online. Try Amazon, Candy Warehouse, or specialty retro candy stores. They are still produced in small amounts by Ferrara Candy Company.

## Summary

**Are Jujubes discontinued?**

**The short answer:**
Not officially, but they're almost impossible to find.

**Key facts:**
- Created in 1920 by the Heide Candy Company
- A movie‑theater staple for decades
- Changed ownership at least four times: Heide → Hershey → Farley's & Sathers → Ferrara
- Still technically produced by Ferrara, but in very limited quantities
- No official discontinuation announcement. They just quietly faded away.

**The legacy:**
Jujubes lasted over 100 years. That's remarkable for a candy that never had a massive marketing budget or a famous spokesperson. They survived on nostalgia, theater culture, and the loyalty of people who loved their stubborn, jaw‑working chew. For a lot of people, finding a box today would feel like finding something from another era. Which, in a way, it would be.
