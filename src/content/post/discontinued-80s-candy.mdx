The 1970s and 1980s were a golden era for candy innovation. From braided caramel ropes to chocolate bars with seven different fillings, candy manufacturers pushed the boundaries of what sugar could do. While some treats survived, many beloved candies disappeared from store shelves and left only memories behind.

Some of these candies started in the 70s and peaked in the 80s. Others were pure 80s creations that came and went within the decade. All of them are missed.

---

## 1. PB Max (1989 to 1994)

**What it was:**
PB Max was Mars's attempt to create the ultimate peanut butter candy bar. It featured whole grain oats, peanut butter, and milk chocolate in a chewy cookie-like base.

**Why we miss it:**
Despite strong sales and a loyal fan base, Mars discontinued PB Max in 1994. The official reason was that the Mars family personally did not like peanut butter. This remains one of the most baffling discontinuations in candy history. Online petitions asking for its return have gathered hundreds of thousands of signatures over the years, but Mars has never brought it back.

**The legacy:**
PB Max is a cautionary tale about letting personal preferences override market demand. It sold well. People loved it. And it was killed anyway.

---

## 2. Bonkers (1985 to 1996)

**What it was:**
Bonkers were chewy fruit candies with an intensely fruity filling. The commercials featured giant fruits literally bonking people on the head, which was peak 80s advertising energy.

**Flavors:**
Grape, watermelon, orange, strawberry, and a chocolate version that surprised everyone.

**Why we miss it:**
Bonkers delivered a fruit flavor explosion that modern fruit chews have never quite matched. The texture was soft and chewy with a liquid center that was unlike anything else on the market at the time.

**Current status:**
Leaf Brands acquired the trademark and briefly brought back Bonkers in 2018. They are hard to find and most fans agree they are not quite the same as the original.

---

## 3. Marathon Bar (1973 to 1981)

**What it was:**
The Marathon Bar was an eight inch braided caramel rope covered in chocolate. The packaging featured a ruler printed on the side so you could measure its length, which was part of the joke and part of the appeal.

**Why we miss it:**
In an era when candy bars were quietly getting smaller, the Marathon Bar stood out as a genuinely generous treat. The braided texture made it fun to eat and it lasted longer than a standard bar.

**Where it lives on:**
The Marathon Bar never really disappeared. It was sold in the UK under the name Curly Wurly, where it is still available today. American fans import them regularly, though they are slightly smaller than the original US version.

---

## 4. Reggie Bar (1978 to 1982)

**What it was:**
Named after baseball legend Reggie Jackson, this candy bar was a round disc of peanuts and caramel covered in chocolate. Think of it as a flatter, wider Baby Ruth.

**Why it was special:**
During Reggie Jackson's first home game at Yankee Stadium after signing with the team, fans threw Reggie Bars onto the field in celebration. It became a genuine cultural moment that no other candy bar has come close to replicating.

**What happened:**
When Jackson's time in the spotlight faded, so did the bar. Standard Brands discontinued it in 1982. It lasted just four years but it left a lasting impression on anyone who was paying attention to baseball in that era.

---

## 5. Choco Lite (1960s to 1993)

**What it was:**
Choco Lite was a chocolate bar with an airy, wafer-thin chocolate interior covered in more chocolate. It was marketed as a lighter alternative to traditional candy bars.

**Why we miss it:**
Choco Lite had a unique melt-in-your-mouth texture that was both crispy and creamy at the same time. It felt indulgent without being heavy. Nabisco discontinued it in 1993, likely due to increasing competition and changing consumer preferences moving away from the "lite" candy category.

---

## 6. Oompas (1971 to 1983)

**What it was:**
Made by the Willy Wonka Candy Company, Oompas were small peanut butter and chocolate discs that came in a tube. They were essentially a proto-Reese's Pieces with a different texture and the Wonka brand adding an extra layer of whimsy.

**Why we miss it:**
Oompas had a peanut butter to chocolate ratio that felt perfectly balanced, and the tube packaging made them easy to carry around. Wonka discontinued them in 1983 to focus on other products in their lineup.

---

## 7. Freshen Up Gum (1975 to 2019)

**What it was:**
Freshen Up was a gum with a liquid gel center that burst in your mouth when you bit down. It came in spearmint, peppermint, cinnamon, and bubble gum flavors.

**Why we miss it:**
The surprise liquid center made every piece feel like an experience rather than just gum. It was particularly popular with kids who loved the burst of flavor. Cadbury Adams discontinued it in 2019 after over four decades of production.

## 8. Astro Pops (1963 to 2004)

**What it was:**
Astro Pops were rocket-shaped lollipops with layers of colorful fruit-flavored hard candy. The pointed shape was designed to look like a rocket, which made them a perfect fit for space-obsessed kids in the 60s, 70s, and 80s.

**Why we miss it:**
Beyond the cool shape, Astro Pops had a three-flavor system where you tasted different flavors as you worked your way through the layers. After a bankruptcy and trademark dispute, Spangler Candy Company revived them in 2010, though many fans say the formula has changed.

---

## 9. Certs (1956 to 2018)

**What it was:**
Certs were breath mints built around an ingredient called Retsyn, a compound of copper and partially hydrogenated cottonseed oil that gave them a distinctive flavor and small green flecks. The tagline was "two mints in one" because they combined breath freshening with actual candy-like taste.

**Why we miss it:**
Certs hit differently than other mints. Stronger than Tic Tacs but not as aggressive as Altoids. Mondelez International discontinued them in 2018 as the breath mint market became oversaturated and newer products crowded them out.

---

## 10. Garbage Can-dy (1980s)

**What it was:**
Capitalizing on the Garbage Pail Kids trading card craze, Garbage Can-dy came in a small plastic trash can filled with sour powder candy. The packaging featured gross-out artwork that matched the trading card aesthetic perfectly.

**Why we miss it:**
Garbage Can-dy represented peak 80s grossness culture. Kids loved the shock value and the sour powder was genuinely good. When the Garbage Pail Kids fad faded in the late 80s, so did the candy. The brand has been revived a few times since but never with the same cultural moment behind it.

---

## 11. Seven Up Bar (1951 to 1979)

**What it was:**
Despite sharing a name with the soda, this candy had nothing to do with it. The Seven Up Bar featured seven separate sections each with a different filling: chocolate, cherry, coconut, fudge, jelly, mint, and orange.

**Why we miss it:**
The Seven Up Bar was a variety pack in a single candy bar. You could eat one section at a time and enjoy different flavors, or bite into multiple sections for unexpected combinations. The Trudeau Candy Company discontinued it in 1979 because having seven different fillings made it too expensive to manufacture profitably. It was a victim of its own ambition.

## Why Did These Candies Disappear?

Most discontinued candy stories come down to a handful of the same reasons.

**Manufacturing costs** were the most common killer. Complex products like the Seven Up Bar and PB Max required specialized equipment and processes. When profit margins tightened, companies cut the products that cost the most to make.

**Corporate consolidation** wiped out dozens of regional candy brands in the 80s and 90s. When a large company acquires a smaller one, legacy products almost always get cut in favor of flagship brands that are easier to scale.

**Shelf space economics** became brutal as retailers started charging slotting fees and limiting how many products they would carry. Niche items with moderate sales lost out to bestsellers every time.

**Changing tastes** also played a role. The bold artificial flavors and experimental textures that defined 80s candy felt dated by the mid-90s. Consumer preferences shifted toward simpler, more natural flavor profiles and many candy makers followed rather than led.

## The Nostalgia Factor

Why do we remember these candies so intensely? A few reasons stand out.

Taste and smell are the most powerful memory triggers the brain has. A similar flavor can pull you back to a specific moment in time more effectively than a photograph. When that flavor disappears, the loss feels surprisingly personal.

There is also a scarcity effect at work. Once something is unavailable, we tend to value it more than we did when it was on every shelf. The candies we could not get anymore became the ones we wanted most.

And for 80s kids specifically, these candies are shared reference points. Talking about PB Max or Bonkers or the Marathon Bar is a way of connecting with people who grew up in the same era. That sense of shared experience keeps the nostalgia alive long after the products themselves are gone.

## Can We Get Them Back?

A few paths exist for the truly dedicated.

Online petitions have gathered real momentum for products like PB Max, and companies do pay attention when the numbers get large enough. Some brands have returned as limited edition releases specifically to test whether demand is still there.

International versions are worth checking. The Marathon Bar lives on as the Curly Wurly in the UK. Some discontinued American candy formulas survive abroad under different names.

Specialty retailers like CandyFavorites.com and OldTimeCandy.com carry hard-to-find and vintage candy, and occasionally have old stock of discontinued products.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Why was PB Max discontinued if it sold well?**
Mars executives reportedly disliked peanut butter personally, which led to the discontinuation despite strong consumer demand. The company has never fully explained the decision publicly.

**Can I still buy Marathon Bars anywhere?**
Yes, under the name Curly Wurly in the UK. They are available through international candy retailers and on Amazon, though slightly smaller than the original US version.

**Are any of these candies coming back?**
Bonkers and Astro Pops have both been revived, though fans debate whether the formulas are the same. Companies occasionally bring back discontinued items as limited editions to test demand.

**Why do discontinued candies taste better in our memories?**
Nostalgia bias plays a significant role. We associate these candies with happy childhood memories which makes them seem better than they might have actually been. Taste preferences also change over time, so a candy we loved at age 10 might not hit the same way at 40.

**Where can I find vintage candy wrappers or packaging?**
eBay and Etsy regularly have vintage candy packaging and collectibles. Dedicated candy nostalgia forums also have active trading communities.
